Section 1

Executive summary

Yahara Comfort Systems, LLC is a residential and light-commercial HVAC services company serving Dane County and adjoining portions of Columbia, Sauk, and Jefferson Counties in south-central Wisconsin from a leased 9,600-square-foot facility in DeForest, north of Madison. Founded in 1998 and operated by its founder since, the Company provides heating and cooling service and repair, system replacement and installation, contracted maintenance, and light-commercial HVAC work for property managers and small facilities.

Revenue grew from $3.41M in FY2023 to $4.12M in FY2025, a 20.7% two-year increase, while seller's discretionary earnings grew 35.3% over the same period, from $556K to $752K. Growth has been organic, driven by replacement volume, steady expansion of the maintenance-agreement base (1,410 active agreements at fiscal year-end 2025, up 34% in two years), and a light-commercial division that has grown to 10% of revenue. Approximately 35% of FY2025 revenue is recurring or semi-recurring, arriving through maintenance agreements and repeat service customers.

The Company fields a staff of 18, including six service technicians and five installers organized in two crews, dispatched through ServiceTitan field-service software adopted in 2022. A service manager and an installation manager run day-to-day field operations; the owner's role is concentrated in general management, replacement sales, and supplier relationships. The owner, 61, is offering the business for sale in anticipation of retirement after 28 years and proposes a 90-day included transition with extended consulting available by agreement.

The opportunity, in brief: an established service base of roughly 8,900 active customers and a 4.8-star public reputation across more than 1,100 Google reviews, positioned in a metropolitan county growing faster than the state; a contracted-maintenance engine that smooths Wisconsin's pronounced heating seasonality; and demonstrated capacity growth with identifiable, unexercised levers — agreement penetration, indoor-air-quality attach services, and the regional heat-pump transition — detailed in Section 10.

Section 3

Business overview & history

The Company was founded in 1998 as a two-person residential furnace-repair shop on Madison's north side. Its founder — a licensed HVAC contractor who remains the sole owner — moved the business to its current DeForest facility in 2004 and has grown it since by a consistent formula: fast, well-reviewed service work that earns replacement installations, and maintenance agreements that keep both coming back. The Company today is a full-service HVAC contractor: it diagnoses and repairs heating and cooling systems, replaces and installs furnaces, air conditioners, heat pumps, and ductless systems, services roughly 1,400 contracted maintenance customers on scheduled visits, and runs a light-commercial division serving property managers, small offices, and retail facilities.

The Company operates as a Wisconsin limited liability company, wholly owned by its founder. It holds the state contractor credentials and EPA certifications described in Section 7.4, maintains no debt other than normal trade payables, and has no affiliated entities. All revenue is earned within its service area; there are no franchise obligations, exclusive supplier commitments, or long-term liabilities that would transfer with the business.

What the buyer acquires, operationally

An acquirer steps into a functioning system rather than a founder's calendar: two field managers run dispatch and installation scheduling through ServiceTitan; six service technicians and two install crews execute a workday averaging 28–34 service calls and 1–2 installations; office staff handle intake, agreement renewals, and collections. The owner's remaining functions — general management, most replacement-sales visits, and supplier relationships — are specific and describable, and Section 8.3 details them honestly, because pricing that role correctly is in every party's interest.

Service area

The primary service area is Dane County — anchored by Madison, Wisconsin's second-largest metro and its fastest-growing county — plus contiguous portions of Columbia, Sauk, and Jefferson Counties reached within roughly 45 minutes of the DeForest shop. The Company does not pursue work beyond this radius; density, not reach, drives its route economics.

Services & revenue mix

4.1  Service & repair — $1,398K FY2025 (33.9%)

Diagnostic and repair work on residential heating and cooling systems, priced flat-rate from the Company's ServiceTitan price book. Demand peaks with Wisconsin's first furnace starts in October–November and the first cooling calls of June; agreement customers receive priority scheduling and a preferred labor rate. Management reports that approximately 68% of service revenue comes from repeat customers or agreement holders, making this line the relationship engine that feeds replacement sales.

4.2  Replacement & installation — $1,812K FY2025 (44.0%)

Residential system replacements — furnaces, air conditioners, dual-fuel pairings, cold-climate heat pumps, and ductless systems — sold predominantly to existing service customers at an average FY2025 ticket of $9,150. The owner performs most in-home sales visits, supported by a ServiceTitan proposal workflow; two install crews complete 1–2 jobs per working day in season. 4.3  Maintenance agreements — $494K FY2025 (12.0%)

1,410 active agreements at $219–$389 per year depending on system count, billed annually or monthly, covering scheduled spring and fall visits, priority dispatch, and repair discounts. Beyond their direct revenue, agreements generate inspection-driven repair and replacement leads and pull service demand into shoulder seasons — their operational value exceeds their line-item revenue.

Rooftop-unit service, replacement, and maintenance for property managers, small offices, retail, and church facilities within the existing service area — the fastest-growing line, up 41% since FY2023 from a small base. Work is won through property-management relationships (including the Company's largest account, Section 6.2) and referrals; the Company deliberately avoids plan-and-spec construction bidding.

Section 5

Market & industry

5.1  A large, fragmented, needs-based industry

US heating and air-conditioning contracting generated an estimated $158.4 billion in 2025 revenue across 118,433 businesses1 — an average of roughly $1.3 million per firm. The narrower North American HVAC services market (maintenance, repair, and replacement, the Company's core) was estimated at $39.4 billion in 2024 and projected to reach $50.4 billion by 2030, a 4.3% compound annual growth rate; repair and replacement is its largest segment (46.2%), and residential its largest end-use (44.8%).2 Census data for the combined plumbing/HVAC contractor code show an average of only about 10.5 employees per establishment.3 No national player holds meaningful share of this market. The Company, at 18 employees and $4.1M revenue, is therefore larger and more systematized than the typical competitor it meets — most of which are owner-operated shops of fewer than ten people.

5.2  Demand drivers touching this service area

An aging installed base. The median age of US owner-occupied homes reached 42 years in 2024, with 47% built before 19804 — and Wisconsin's housing stock skews older still, with roughly 59% of homes built before 1980.5 Older homes hold older systems: replacement demand in the Company's service area is a function of installed-base age, not of consumer sentiment, and the Company's 8,900-customer database is effectively a map of that aging equipment.

A heating-dominant climate. Madison averages 7,138 heating degree days per year against only 627 cooling degree days6 — heating demand roughly eleven times cooling demand. In this climate a failed furnace in January is not a deferrable purchase, which is why the Company's service line holds firm through economic cycles and why its winter emergency capacity, prioritized for agreement holders, converts reliably into replacement work.

The electrification cycle — tailwind with an honest asterisk. Heat pumps have outsold gas furnaces nationally every year since 2021, by about 12% in 2025 (≈3.6M heat pumps vs ≈3.2M gas furnaces shipped).7 The federal 25C tax credit that subsidized this shift (30%, up to $2,000 per heat pump) expired December 31, 2025, which pulled some replacement demand forward into 2025 — a headwind buyers should weigh for 2026 replacement volumes.8 What remains active locally: Wisconsin's Focus on Energy program is funding 2026 residential heat-pump rebates of up to roughly $1,000 plus instant equipment discounts,9 and income-qualified federal HEAR rebates continue where state funds remain. The Company began dual-fuel and cold-climate heat-pump installations at scale in 2024 — dual-fuel configurations being the natural fit for a 7,000-heating-degree-day climate — and management reports heat pumps featured in roughly one in five FY2025 replacement proposals.

The refrigerant transition supports both repair and replacement economics. As of January 2025, new residential systems can no longer be manufactured with R-410A refrigerant; successor A2L equipment has run roughly 10–15% more expensive.10 An EPA rule amended in May 2026 allows remaining pre-2025 R-410A inventory to be installed until depleted,11 while servicing existing R-410A systems remains legal indefinitely — with service-refrigerant prices expected to rise as supply tightens. For an operator like the Company, the transition raises average replacement tickets, increases the value of diagnostic honesty on repair-versus-replace decisions, and rewards shops whose technicians are already A2L-trained, as the Company's are.

Skilled labor is the industry's binding constraint — and a seller's asset. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects HVAC technician employment to grow 8% from 2024 to 2034, much faster than the average occupation, with about 40,100 openings per year;12 industry estimates put the current technician shortfall near 110,000.13 In a market where the scarce input is a trained, licensed bench, the Company's eleven field employees — with median field tenure of six years and two in-house state-licensed qualifiers — are as much a part of what conveys as the customer list.

5.3  The local market

Dane County is Wisconsin's fastest-growing county: approximately 590,000 residents in 2025, projected to reach 887,000 by 2050 — growth requiring an estimated 160,000 additional housing units.14 The City of Madison alone added 2,328 net new homes in 2025, above its ten-year average, with 5,320 more under construction at year-end.15 Every unit added is a future service and replacement customer; every unit aged is a nearer-term one. Wisconsin also maintains a genuine licensing barrier to entry: HVAC contractor registration plus an individual qualifier credential requiring four years of documented experience, and a separate dwelling-contractor credential for one- and two-family work16 — a moat around incumbents with licensed staff.

5.4  Buyer demand for businesses like the Company

Consolidation capital has moved decisively into residential HVAC services: private-equity add-on acquisitions rose 88% year-over-year through mid-2025, when PE-backed buyers took more than half of sector transactions for the first time,17 and one M&A advisory describes residential HVAC as "midway through its consolidation cycle."18 This Memorandum offers no opinion of value; the observation is narrower — well-run local operators with recurring service revenue are the specific asset this capital seeks, and the reader is one of many parties for whom that is true.

Section 6

Customers & sales

6.1  Customer base

The Company's ServiceTitan database holds approximately 8,900 active customers — parties invoiced at least once in the trailing 36 months — of which 1,410 hold maintenance agreements. Revenue is approximately 85% residential and 15% commercial. The residential customer is typically an owner-occupant of a single-family home in Dane County's suburbs and exurbs; the commercial base is property managers, small offices, retail, and congregations, served under maintenance contracts or posted-rate service.

6.2  Concentration

Customer concentration is negligible by design. The largest account — a regional property-management firm under continuous contract for nine years — represented 5.8% of FY2025 revenue; the top five accounts together represented 14.2%. No other account exceeded 2% of revenue. The loss of any single relationship would be absorbed within a normal quarter's demand variation.

6.3  How business arrives

Management attributes new-customer acquisition, in order of volume, to: word-of-mouth referral and the Company's 4.8-star Google profile (1,100+ reviews); paid search and local-services ads (FY2025 marketing spend of $103K, 2.5% of revenue); the agreement base's own referral behavior; and property-manager relationships in the commercial line. There is no outside sales force. The replacement pipeline is fed principally by service technicians' inspection findings and agreement-visit condition reports — a structural advantage of the maintenance base that a buyer inherits intact.

6.4  Reputation & brand

The Company's public reputation is its principal marketing asset: a 4.8-star Google Business rating across more than 1,100 reviews accumulated since 2012, a Better Business Bureau A+ accreditation, and a referral pattern management describes as self-sustaining in its core zip codes. Operations, facilities & systems

7.1  How a day runs

Service demand arrives by phone and web form to two customer-service representatives, who book against technician skill and zone in ServiceTitan; the service manager builds and adjusts the day's routes. Technicians run flat-rate diagnostics from company iPads, present options from the price book, collect payment on-site, and flag replacement candidates to the sales pipeline. Installation jobs sold by the owner are engineered by the install manager (load calculation, equipment ordering through two regional distributors, permit filing) and executed by two-person crews, typically within two to three weeks of sale in season. Agreement visits are batch-scheduled into shoulder months by the CSRs from ServiceTitan's membership module. Invoicing, payroll, and financial reporting run through QuickBooks Online, reconciled by a part-time bookkeeper.

7.2  Facility

The Company leases a 9,600-square-foot facility in DeForest — office, warehouse with racked equipment and parts inventory, sheet-metal area, and fenced yard — from an unrelated third-party landlord under a lease running through 2028 with a renewal option; current rent is approximately $84K per year. The location sits eight minutes from the I-39/90/94 interchange, placing the whole service area within a 45-minute radius. The landlord has indicated willingness to assign the lease to a qualified buyer. [BROKER: confirm assignment terms]

9.2  What moved, and why

Revenue grew 9.7% in FY2024 and 10.0% in FY2025. The growth is broad-based across all four lines (table, Section 4), led in percentage terms by maintenance agreements (+32.8% over two years) and light-commercial (+40.8%), and in dollar terms by replacement (+$270K). Management attributes FY2025 replacement strength partly to customers accelerating purchases ahead of the federal 25C credit's December 2025 expiration — the pull-forward noted honestly in Section 5.2 — and partly to A2L-transition equipment pricing lifting average tickets.

Gross margin improved 1.1 points over two years, to 42.0% — within published industry ranges for a mixed service/replacement shop — as the revenue mix shifted toward higher-margin service and agreement work and flat-rate pricing discipline held through equipment cost increases. Operating expenses grew slower than revenue (13.9% versus 20.7% cumulatively), with marketing the only line deliberately grown faster; the resulting operating leverage carried operating margin from 8.1% to 11.1% and SDE from $556K to $752K.

9.4  Recurring revenue & seasonality in the numbers

Maintenance agreements produced $494K of FY2025 revenue directly (12.0% of total) at a reported renewal rate near 88%. Adding the share of service revenue management attributes to repeat customers and agreement holders (approximately 68% of the service line), roughly 35% of FY2025 revenue is recurring or semi-recurring — earned from parties with an existing, active relationship rather than from first-time demand. Management estimates 57% of revenue is earned October–March; the agreement base's shoulder-season tune-up visits are the principal reason technician utilization holds through April–May and September, and the visits themselves originate much of each peak season's repair and replacement pipeline.

Section 10

Growth opportunities

Each opportunity below is an action the current owner has identified but not pursued, with the reason stated. None is priced into the presentation of historical earnings; the buyer underwrites the upside.

10.1  Agreement penetration

The Company holds 1,410 agreements against roughly 8,900 active customers — a 16% penetration rate, while industry survey data place high-performing shops' membership attach rates at 30–50% of jobs.a At the FY2025 average agreement value of approximately $350, each additional point of penetration represents roughly $31K of direct recurring revenue, before the repair and replacement flow agreement visits generate. The owner attributes the gap to deliberate pacing: tune-up capacity was constrained until the apprentice pipeline matured in 2023–24, and the Company has never run a systematic enrollment campaign against its own service history — the CRM data to do so sits in ServiceTitan today.

10.2  Indoor air quality & duct services attach

IAQ products (filtration, humidification, UV) and duct sealing/cleaning currently arrive only when customers ask — under 2% of revenue, unmeasured as a line. The installed base of aging homes (Section 5.2) is the natural market, the service visit is the natural sales moment, and the equipment is stocked by the Company's existing distributors. The owner, by his own account, never built the technician scripting or pricing menu to sell it systematically.

10.3  The electrification transition

Heat pumps featured in roughly one in five FY2025 replacement proposals; national shipment trends (Section 5.2) and Wisconsin's active 2026 rebate programsb indicate the mix will grow. The Company's early position — certified technicians, dual-fuel install experience since 2024 — is ahead of typical main-street competitors. Marketing that position (the current website does not mention heat pumps) is an untaken step a buyer with marketing capability executes immediately.

10.4  Hours coverage

The Company dispatches Monday–Friday 7:00–17:00, with after-hours emergency service reserved for agreement holders. Weekend and evening availability — standard among the market's larger competitors — is an unserved slice of the Company's own call log: management reports the CSRs decline or defer roughly 15–20 calls per month outside dispatch hours in season. Extending coverage requires an on-call rotation the current bench could staff with one additional technician hire; the owner declined to add weekend obligations in his final operating years, a constraint that retires with him.

10.5  What is deliberately not claimed

This section makes no claims about geographic expansion, acquisition roll-ins, or commercial plan-and-spec bidding. Each is possible; none has factual support in the Company's operating history, and a document that projects them would be selling the buyer's own homework back at a markup. The four opportunities above share one property: the demand signal already exists in the Company's systems, and only the execution is missing.
